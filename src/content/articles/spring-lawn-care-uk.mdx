Spring is the most important time of year for a UK lawn. What you do (or don't do) between February and May determines whether you spend the summer enjoying a thick green sward or staring at a patchy yellow disappointment.

This is the timeline I follow on my own lawn, refined over several seasons of getting things wrong before getting them right.

## February: watch and wait

Resist the urge to do anything heavy. The grass is still dormant, the soil is cold, and any traffic compacts wet ground. The single useful job this month is a light tidy: pick up fallen branches, brush off worm casts on a dry day, and make sure your mower is serviced and your blades are sharp before March demand kicks in.

If you're going to test your soil pH (worth doing once every few years), February is the time. UK lawns generally do best between 6.0 and 6.5. Anything below 5.5 and moss will outcompete grass no matter what else you do.

## March: the first cut and a gentle wake-up

Once the lawn has visibly started growing, give it a first cut on a dry day with the mower set high. Never take more than a third of the leaf in one go, especially on the first cut of the year. Cutting too short in March is the single most common mistake I see.

This is also a reasonable time for a light spring feed, but only if growth has clearly started. Feeding dormant grass is a waste. A balanced spring feed (high nitrogen, lower phosphorus and potassium) will green things up within ten days. UK options include Westland SafeLawn, Aftercut All In One, and MOOWY's spring blend.

## April: scarify, aerate, overseed

This is the heavy work month, and it's where most lawns are made or broken.

If your lawn has thatch (the spongy layer of dead organic matter at the base of the grass) more than about 1cm deep, scarify it. Hand rakes work for small lawns but are punishing on the back. For anything over 50 square metres, an electric scarifier earns its keep within one season. The Bosch AVR 1100 and Einhell GC-SC 36/35 Li are the two I'd genuinely consider.

After scarifying, aerate compacted areas. A simple hollow-tine fork is fine for small lawns. The pulled cores can be left to break down or raked off, and the holes filled with a sandy top-dressing if you want to improve drainage.

Then overseed any thin or bare patches. Use a UK-appropriate seed mix matched to your usage: hard-wearing rye-fescue blends for family lawns, finer fescue-bent mixes for ornamental lawns. Water the seeded areas daily until germination (10-14 days), then keep the surface moist for another fortnight.

## May: settle into the rhythm

By May the lawn should be thickening up. Mowing frequency increases — typically once a week, sometimes twice in warm weeks. Drop the cutting height gradually, never below about 25mm for a family lawn.

Watch for pests and diseases. Leatherjackets and chafer grubs do their damage in spring; if you see crows or magpies persistently digging, that's your signal. A second feed in late May, lighter than the spring one, keeps growth steady into summer.

By the end of May, if you've done the work, the lawn should be thick, dense, and ready for the abuse of summer.

## What I'd skip

A few things UK lawn content recommends that I genuinely think are a waste of time and money for most home lawns:

- **Iron-only "moss killer" before tackling the underlying cause.** Moss is a symptom of compaction, shade, low pH, or poor drainage. Kill the moss without fixing those and it'll be back by autumn.
- **Dethatching every year regardless of need.** If thatch is under 1cm, leave it alone.
- **Premium "professional" seed mixes for ordinary lawns.** A good rye-fescue blend from a specialist UK supplier is plenty. You're not building a bowling green.

Spring done well sets up everything else. Get February to May right and the rest of the year is mostly maintenance.
